# Entrepreneurship & Enterprise Development

## Developing an Entrepreneurial Mindset

Students should be able to articulate who they are and what they value, identify the personal competencies that matter for entrepreneurs, and recognise what separates innovation from ordinary work. A strong student does not just list traits from a textbook — they can describe their own values, name their own competency gaps, and point to a specific unmet need they have noticed in their community.

This module is based on the TEVETA-approved Skills Award in Entrepreneurship and Enterprise Development for Startups (EEDS) syllabus. To make the concepts concrete, examples throughout follow Bwalya Tembo, a recent graduate of a Food Production programme at Lusaka Vocational and Technical College who is exploring a small business making peanut butter and groundnut snacks for local schools.

### Discovering Your Identity and Personal Values

Before you start a business, you need to know what you stand for. Your values shape every decision — who you hire, what corners you refuse to cut, how you respond to setbacks.

A useful exercise is the Tree of Life, where you map yourself onto a tree:
- Roots — where you come from (family, community, training, language, culture)
- Trunk — your core skills and competencies right now
- Branches — your hopes and dreams for the future
- Leaves — the people who support and influence you
- Fruit — the contributions you want to make to others

Doing this exercise honestly takes about 30 minutes and gives most students something they did not have before — a grounded sense of why they are pursuing entrepreneurship in the first place.

A related exercise is the Story of Self — a 2-minute story about a specific moment that shaped why you care about your business idea.

Weak Story of Self: "I want to start a snack business because there is good demand and I am hardworking."

Strong Story of Self (Bwalya): "When I was 12, my mother sold groundnut snacks at the Kabwata market to pay my school fees. I noticed her snacks sold faster than anyone else's because she added the right amount of salt and roasted them properly. That was my first lesson in why product quality matters. Today I want to take what she did and turn it into a small enterprise that can supply schools across Lusaka."

The strong version is grounded in a specific scene and a specific lesson learned. "I want to start a business" is not a Story of Self. The market scene is.

### What Is Entrepreneurship — and What It Isn't

An entrepreneur is someone who identifies an opportunity, organises resources, takes calculated risks, and creates value through a business venture. There are several types:
- **Necessity entrepreneurs** — start a business because they cannot find employment
- **Opportunity entrepreneurs** — start a business because they see a market gap
- **Innovative entrepreneurs** — create new products, services, or business models
- **Social entrepreneurs** — focus on solving a social or environmental problem
- **Lifestyle entrepreneurs** — build a business that supports the life they want, not necessarily growth

Most TEVET startups in Zambia begin as necessity or opportunity entrepreneurs. There is no shame in that — but knowing your type helps you make decisions that fit your situation.

Common misconception: "Entrepreneurs are born, not made." The competencies that define successful entrepreneurs are learnable. What separates those who succeed is not personality — it is habit and practice.

### Why Entrepreneurship Matters

Entrepreneurship is worth the difficulty for several reasons:
- **Job creation** — a successful enterprise employs others, multiplying impact beyond the founder
- **Income and wealth-building** — for those willing to take the risk, business ownership can generate income that wage employment cannot
- **Community development** — local enterprises keep money circulating locally and respond to needs that distant suppliers ignore
- **Personal growth** — running a business builds skills (leadership, finance, problem-solving) faster than most jobs
- **Innovation** — startups are usually where new products, services, and methods first appear
- **Independence** — control over how you work, what you build, and the values you operate by

For TEVET graduates specifically, entrepreneurship is often the most realistic route to applying technical training when formal employment is scarce. A trained electrician who starts a small wiring contractor business creates work for themselves and, eventually, for others — applying every part of their training in the process.

### Personal Entrepreneurial Competencies — The Three Clusters

TEVETA groups entrepreneurial competencies into three clusters. A strong student can self-rate honestly on each one.

**Achievement Cluster** — getting things done:
- Opportunity-seeking — actively looking for problems to solve
- Persistence — continuing when others give up
- Commitment to work — finishing what you start, on time
- Demand for quality and efficiency — work that meets a standard
- Risk-taking — acting under uncertainty without being reckless

**Planning Cluster** — thinking ahead:
- Goal setting — specific, measurable targets
- Information seeking — researching before deciding
- Systematic planning and monitoring — breaking work into steps and tracking progress

**Power Cluster** — influencing others:
- Persuasion and networking — building a case and bringing people along
- Self-confidence — backing yourself, even when challenged
- Integrity — keeping promises and staying honest under pressure

A self-rating exercise: rate yourself 1–5 on each of the eleven competencies above. Be honest. Your two lowest scores are not personality flaws — they are specific skills you now know to practise. A student who completes this honestly leaves with a personal development plan, not just a list.

### Innovation and Opportunity Recognition

Innovation in a Zambian context rarely means inventing something never seen before. It usually means:
- Improving an existing product (Bwalya's snacks are lower-sugar and longer-shelf-life than competitors')
- Reaching an underserved customer (delivering to schools that mostly buy from one supplier)
- Combining existing things in a new way (small re-sealable sachets sized for school lunch boxes)
- Lowering cost or increasing convenience (mobile-money payment instead of cash)

The traits of innovative entrepreneurs: curiosity, willingness to experiment, comfort with imperfect first attempts, and the discipline to listen to customer feedback rather than fall in love with their own idea.

### Leadership, Networking, and Business Ethics

Even a one-person business has leadership — over yourself, your finances, and the people you work with. Effective entrepreneurial leaders set the standard with their own behaviour, build networks deliberately, and adhere to business ethics — keeping promises, paying suppliers on time, and refusing to participate in corruption.

This last point matters even more in early-stage Zambian businesses than at large companies. Your reputation is one of your only assets when you are starting out — and it can be destroyed by a single shortcut.

## Establishing an Enterprise

Students should be able to take a raw idea and pressure-test whether it is actually a business. A strong student leaves this section able to describe their business model in nine elements (the Business Model Canvas) and name the legal steps needed to register and operate in Zambia.

### Generating Business Ideas

Good business ideas come from four main sources:
1. **Personal experience** — a problem you have faced (Bwalya's mother's market stall)
2. **Community needs** — gaps you observe (no reliable supplier of healthy snacks at local schools)
3. **Skills and training** — what you can already do well
4. **Trends** — sectors that are growing (Zambia's expanding agro-processing demand)

Weak idea generation: "I want to start a chicken business because everyone is doing chicken."

Strong idea generation: "I have observed that primary schools in my area buy break-time snacks but their current supplier delivers irregularly and the snacks are too sweet. I have food production training and access to my mother's roasting equipment. I want to test whether I can supply healthier snacks more reliably."

The strong version names a specific problem, a specific customer, and the entrepreneur's specific edge.

### Identifying a Viable Opportunity

Not every idea is a business. To screen an opportunity, ask:
- Is there real, paying demand? (Have you spoken to potential customers, or only assumed?)
- Can you reach those customers at a cost that still leaves you a profit?
- What is the legal and regulatory environment? (Some sectors require licences you cannot yet get.)
- What factors could kill the business — price competition, supply disruption, weather, exchange rate?

Bwalya's screen: He visited four schools and asked head teachers what they currently spend on break-time snacks (ZMW 800–1500 per week per school) and what frustrates them about current suppliers (irregular delivery, too sweet, not portion-controlled). He confirmed there was paying demand and a gap. He also learned that schools require a food handler's permit from the local council — a legal requirement he must meet before he can sell.

### Building a Business Model — The Nine Elements

A business model is how your enterprise creates, delivers, and captures value. The Business Model Canvas defines nine elements:

1. **Value proposition** — What problem do you solve and why is your solution better?
2. **Customer segments** — Exactly who are your customers? (Not "everyone" — that is a sign you have not thought hard enough.)
3. **Customer relationships** — How do you acquire, keep, and grow customers?
4. **Channels** — How do you reach customers? (Direct delivery, retail, online, agents)
5. **Revenue streams** — How do you make money? (Per-unit sales, subscriptions, contracts)
6. **Key resources** — What do you need? (Equipment, premises, working capital, people)
7. **Key activities** — What do you have to do? (Production, sales, deliveries, accounting)
8. **Key partnerships** — Who do you depend on? (Suppliers, distributors, regulators)
9. **Cost structure** — What does it cost to run? (Fixed costs, variable costs)

Bwalya's canvas (compressed):
- Value proposition — portion-controlled, low-sugar groundnut snacks delivered weekly to school tuck shops
- Segments — primary schools in Lusaka with 200–500 pupils
- Channels — direct delivery to school administrators
- Revenue — ZMW 5 per unit, minimum 100 units per school per week
- Key resources — roasting equipment (his mother's), packaging machine (rented), small van (uncle's)
- Key partnerships — groundnut farmers in Mumbwa, packaging supplier in Lusaka
- Cost structure — raw groundnuts and packaging are variable; fuel and rent are fixed

A student who completes this section should be able to draft their own canvas in under an hour.

### Writing a Business Plan

A Business Model Canvas is a one-page summary. A business plan is the longer document that fleshes each element out with detail, evidence, and a timeline. Banks, CEEC grant reviewers, and most government schemes require a written business plan before they will commit money.

A typical Zambian small-business plan has these sections:

1. **Executive summary** — one page that captures the entire plan; usually written last, even though it appears first
2. **Business description** — what the business does, legal structure, location, ownership
3. **Market analysis** — who the customers are, market size, competitors, and what makes you different
4. **Products and services** — what you sell and how it is produced
5. **Marketing and sales plan** — how you reach customers, pricing, distribution, promotion
6. **Operations plan** — production process, suppliers, equipment, premises, staffing
7. **Management team** — who runs the business and what they bring
8. **Financial plan** — start-up costs, projected income statement, cash flow forecast, break-even analysis (typically three years)
9. **Funding request** (if seeking finance) — how much you need, how you will use it, repayment terms
10. **Appendices** — registration certificates, CVs, supplier letters, market research

Bwalya's first business plan was 14 pages. Most of his time went into the financial plan — building three years of projected income with realistic month-by-month sales assumptions and matching the expense lines to his Business Model Canvas cost structure. The CEEC reviewer later told him his application stood out because the financial assumptions were defensible — he could explain where every number came from.

A weak business plan reads like an essay. A strong business plan reads like an answer to the specific questions an investor or lender will ask.

### Legal Requirements in Zambia

To operate legally as a Zambian business you need to consider:
- **PACRA** (Patents and Companies Registration Agency) — register a business name (sole proprietor) or incorporate a limited company
- **ZRA** (Zambia Revenue Authority) — register for a Tax Identification Number (TPIN) and the appropriate tax type (turnover tax, VAT, etc.)
- **Local council** — trading licence, signage permit, and sector-specific permits (e.g. food handler's permit for food businesses)
- **NAPSA** (National Pension Scheme Authority) — register for pension contributions if you have employees
- **Sector regulators** — depending on your industry (e.g. ZABS for product standards, ZEMA for environmental compliance)

Skipping these is not a shortcut. Many small businesses get shut down — or denied larger contracts — because they tried to operate informally and got caught later. Most larger Zambian buyers (schools, NGOs, government, established companies) screen suppliers for proper registration.

### Types of Enterprise

When you register, you choose a structure:
- **Sole proprietorship** — simple, low-cost, but you are personally liable for all debts
- **Partnership** — shared ownership, shared liability between partners
- **Private limited company (Ltd)** — more expensive to set up and run, but liability is limited to the company's assets

Most Zambian startups begin as sole proprietorships and convert later. The trade-off is ease of setup against personal risk.

## Managing an Enterprise

Students should be able to describe how a small enterprise mobilises resources, tracks money, avoids corruption, and builds networks. A strong student understands that running a business is mostly disciplined repetition — not heroic decisions.

### Mobilising Resources — Financial and Non-Financial

Financial sources for Zambian startups:
- Personal savings (the most common starting point)
- Family and friends (often informal — agree terms in writing anyway)
- Microfinance institutions (e.g. Madison Finance, FINCA Zambia)
- Banks (usually require collateral or trading history)
- Government schemes — CEEC (Citizens Economic Empowerment Commission), CDF (Constituency Development Fund grants for local businesses)
- Grants and competitions — NYDC, BongoHive, donor-funded programmes

Each source has a different cost, speed, and risk. To screen and select the right one, ask:
- **How much do you need, and for what?** — Working capital for stock has different requirements than capital for equipment
- **How quickly do you need it?** — Personal savings and family are immediate; banks and CEEC can take months
- **What can you afford to repay?** — Bank loans charge interest; equity (selling a share of your business) charges ownership; grants charge nothing in money but often impose reporting and compliance
- **What collateral or track record do you have?** — Banks usually require both; microfinance often only one; family requires neither but can damage relationships if mishandled
- **What conditions are attached?** — A grant may require quarterly reports, audited accounts, or a specific use of funds. Read the terms before accepting

For a typical TEVET startup, the practical screening order is: personal savings first (test the idea cheaply), then family or microfinance (small expansion), then CEEC or banks (once you have records that prove the business works). Skipping straight to a bank loan with no trading history almost always fails — and damages your credit profile if you default.

Non-financial resources are often more important than money:
- Equipment loans or shared use (Bwalya uses his mother's roaster rather than buying his own)
- Mentorship — a former teacher, a successful local entrepreneur
- Networks and introductions
- Free training — TEVETA, NYDC, donor programmes

A common mistake is to treat fundraising as the first step. The first step is testing whether anyone will pay for your product — funding is far easier to find once you can show demand.

### Managing Finances — Records, Budgets, Cash Flow

Every business owner — not just the accountant — needs to understand:

**Record-keeping.** Every sale, every purchase, every expense, written down on the day it happens. A simple notebook works. A spreadsheet works better. The point is consistency, not the tool.

Weak record-keeping: "I think we sold about 200 units last week — business is going well."

Strong record-keeping: "Week of 5 May 2026: Sold 235 units across 4 schools. Cost of goods ZMW 587. Revenue ZMW 1,175. Gross margin ZMW 588."

The weak version cannot tell you whether the business is profitable. The strong version can answer in 10 seconds.

The records most small Zambian businesses need:
- **Sales journal** — date, customer, item, quantity, unit price, total, payment method
- **Cash book** — daily record of cash and mobile-money received and paid out
- **Purchases journal** — every purchase from suppliers (date, supplier, item, cost, payment status)
- **Receipts and invoices** — issue a numbered receipt for every sale; keep copies of every invoice you issue or receive
- **Stock register** — what you have in inventory, opening balance, additions, sales, closing balance
- **Expense ledger** — fuel, rent, electricity, transport, packaging, repairs, with receipts attached where possible
- **Payroll record** (if you have employees) — hours, gross pay, deductions, NAPSA, PAYE, net pay

For the first year, the simplest approach is one notebook per record type, plus a folder for invoices and receipts. As volume grows, move to a spreadsheet. Accounting software is usually unnecessary in the first 12 months — the discipline of writing things down matters more than the tool.

**Budgeting.** A simple monthly budget has four parts:
1. Expected income — realistic, not optimistic
2. Fixed costs — rent, salaries, loan repayments (do not change with sales)
3. Variable costs — materials, packaging, fuel (rise with sales)
4. Savings and reinvestment — money set aside for growth and emergencies

**Cash flow.** This is what kills small businesses more often than profit does. You can be profitable on paper and still close down because you ran out of cash to pay suppliers this week. Track money in and money out separately from profit.

**Separating personal and business finances.** Different bank accounts. Different mobile-money lines if possible. Pay yourself a fixed monthly amount — do not "borrow" from the business. Mixing the two is the most common reason small business owners cannot tell whether they are actually making money.

### Ethics and Anti-Corruption

The Anti-Corruption Commission (ACC) is the Zambian agency responsible for preventing and prosecuting corruption. As a small business owner you will encounter corruption-prone situations:
- Pressure to pay bribes for permits or licences
- Requests for "facilitation fees" to win contracts
- Suggestions to under-report income to lower your tax bill
- Kickbacks from suppliers in exchange for orders

Penalties for corruption convictions under Zambian law include heavy fines and imprisonment — and a corruption conviction will end most professional careers.

Practical anti-corruption habits for a small business:
- Refuse to pay bribes, even small ones (every paid bribe makes the next one easier)
- Keep clean records — clean records make corruption harder to hide
- Pay your taxes honestly, even when others around you do not
- Speak up when pressured — including to the ACC if necessary
- Pay suppliers and employees fairly and on time

This is not just ethics for its own sake. Zambian buyers — particularly larger ones — increasingly screen suppliers for compliance. A clean business has access to contracts a corrupt one cannot win.

### Building Business Networks

A network is not a contact list. It is a set of relationships you have invested in over time.

Practical network-building for a small business:
- Join an industry association (e.g. ZCSMBA — Zambia Chamber of Small and Medium Business Association)
- Attend trade shows, district business forums, and TEVETA-organised events
- Maintain three to five mentor relationships — people more experienced than you whom you can call for advice
- Be useful to others before you need them — introductions, recommendations, small favours

Bwalya joined a Lusaka small-food-producer WhatsApp group, attended one BongoHive event per month, and maintained a quarterly check-in with a retired food technologist who had run a similar business in the 1990s. Over six months, three of his first contracts came from network introductions.

A network is only useful if it produces something. Once a quarter, evaluate whether yours is delivering:
- How many useful introductions or referrals did your network produce in the past three months?
- How many of your last five customers came directly or indirectly from a network connection?
- Which contacts and groups generated value? Which consumed time but produced nothing?
- Are you giving back enough? (Networks deteriorate quickly when one party only takes.)

Honest evaluation usually reveals that one or two relationships are doing most of the work. Invest more in those, and politely scale back the obligations that are not paying off. A small, active network beats a long contact list every time.

## Designing a Product and Production Process

Students should be able to walk through how a product gets from raw materials to a paying customer, design a basic prototype, and calculate a price that actually covers costs. A strong student can identify two specific cost-reduction opportunities in their own product.

### Assessing the Value Chain

A value chain is the full sequence from raw inputs to the customer:
1. **Sourcing** — where you get materials
2. **Production** — how you make the product
3. **Distribution** — how you get it to the customer
4. **After-sales** — support, replacements, follow-up

For each stage, ask: who are the stakeholders? What can go wrong? Where can I reduce cost or improve quality?

Bwalya's value chain analysis identified two specific problems: groundnut prices spiked during the rainy season (sourcing risk) and weekly deliveries to schools wasted fuel (distribution inefficiency). His solutions: a forward-contract with a farmer in Mumbwa for the rainy-season months, and consolidation of deliveries from five days to two. Result: 18% cost reduction in three months.

### Producing Products that Satisfy Customers

The new product development (NPD) process:
1. Idea generation — what problems are customers describing?
2. Concept development — what would the product look like?
3. Prototype — build a rough version (does not need to be pretty)
4. Test with real customers — give it to them, watch what happens, ask what they would change
5. Refine — improve based on feedback
6. Launch — produce at full scale

Common production processes: batch production (most TEVET startups), continuous production (large factories), job/custom production (one-off orders).

The factors that determine customer satisfaction are quality, consistency, price, packaging, reliability of supply, and after-sales handling. A weak business optimises one of these. A strong business identifies which two or three matter most to its specific customers and gets those right consistently.

### Designing a Prototype Through Design Thinking

Design thinking is a five-step process for designing products customers actually want:
1. **Empathise** — observe and talk to real customers
2. **Define** — describe the specific problem in their words
3. **Ideate** — generate many possible solutions
4. **Prototype** — build the cheapest, fastest version that demonstrates the idea
5. **Test** — give it to users and learn from what they actually do, not what they say they would do

A prototype is not a finished product. It is the cheapest thing that demonstrates the core idea well enough to test it.

Bwalya's first prototype: 50 hand-packed sachets of roasted groundnuts taken to one school for a one-week trial. He learned that sachets needed to be smaller (children could not finish a 100g pack at break time) and that schools wanted a re-sealable design. The prototype cost him ZMW 200 to test. A polished launch without that test would have cost ZMW 20,000 and likely failed for the same reasons.

### Managing the Product Life Cycle

Every product moves through stages:
1. **Introduction** — launching, slow sales, high cost per unit
2. **Growth** — sales rise, awareness builds, cost per unit falls
3. **Maturity** — sales stabilise, competition increases, margins thin
4. **Decline** — demand drops, time to innovate, refresh, or move on

The marketing you do at introduction (educate the customer) is different from what you do in maturity (defend market share). Knowing where your product sits matters for every decision around pricing, promotion, and reinvestment.

### Costing and Pricing

Costing answers: what does it actually cost to produce one unit?
- Direct materials (groundnuts, salt, packaging)
- Direct labour (your time, helper's wages)
- Manufacturing overhead (fuel for the roaster, share of rent)

Pricing answers: what should I charge?
- **Cost-plus pricing** — total cost per unit + profit margin
- **Market-based pricing** — what competitors charge, adjusted for your differentiation
- **Value-based pricing** — what the customer is willing to pay for the benefit you provide

Common pricing mistakes:
- Pricing below full cost to attract customers (you lose money on every sale and the business cannot survive)
- Forgetting to include your own time as a cost
- Not adjusting prices when input costs rise (e.g. when groundnut prices spike)
- Pricing identically to competitors despite a meaningfully better product

**Methods of minimising production cost.** Cutting cost without cutting quality is one of the most direct ways to grow a small business. Practical methods:
- **Bulk purchasing** — buy raw materials in larger quantities to lower the per-unit price (Bwalya consolidated his groundnut purchasing from weekly to monthly and saved 12% on materials)
- **Supplier negotiation** — once you have a track record of consistent orders, ask for better prices or longer payment terms
- **Process efficiency** — reduce wasted time and motion (batching tasks, organising the workspace, standardising repeated steps)
- **Waste reduction** — track scrap and rework; every unit thrown away is paid-for material that earned no revenue
- **Energy and fuel** — schedule deliveries to consolidate trips, switch off equipment when not in use, maintain machines to keep them efficient
- **Outsourcing where cheaper** — sometimes a specialist (e.g. a packaging printer) can produce at lower cost than doing it yourself
- **Forward contracts** — lock in prices for inputs that fluctuate seasonally (Bwalya's rainy-season groundnut contract)

Cost reduction is not the same as cutting corners. Reducing costs by lowering quality usually costs more in the long run through customer loss and reputation damage.

Bwalya's costing for one 50g sachet:
- Groundnuts: ZMW 1.20
- Salt and oil: ZMW 0.10
- Packaging: ZMW 0.40
- Labour (his time, allocated): ZMW 0.50
- Overhead allocation: ZMW 0.30
- **Total cost: ZMW 2.50**
- **Selling price: ZMW 5.00**
- **Profit per unit: ZMW 2.50**

He sells 1000 units per week. Gross profit ZMW 2,500/week. From that he must still cover transport, his own basic salary, and any savings.

A student who completes this topic should be able to do this calculation for their own product without prompting.

## Business Pitching

Students should be able to deliver a 5-minute pitch that names a specific problem, a specific customer, and a credible plan. A strong student can answer hard financial questions on the spot — not because they memorised numbers, but because they actually know their business.

### Building a Pitch Deck

A pitch deck for a startup typically includes:
1. **The problem** — what specific pain point are you addressing?
2. **Your solution** — what do you offer and why is it better?
3. **Target market** — who are your customers and how big is the opportunity?
4. **Business model** — how do you make money?
5. **Traction** — what evidence do you have that this is working? (sales, contracts, pilot results)
6. **Team** — who is involved and why are you the right people?
7. **Financials** — projected revenue, costs, break-even
8. **The ask** — what specifically do you need? (money, partnership, mentorship, customers)

The first slide is the most important. If your problem is not clear in 30 seconds, the rest does not matter.

### The Founder's Dream — Personal and Quantified

A pitch deck should communicate not just what you do, but why you specifically are doing it. The founder's dream has two parts:
- **Personal** — your story and motivation (your Story of Self from the first topic)
- **Quantified** — what you want to build, in numbers (e.g. "supply 100 schools across Lusaka by 2028, generating ZMW 10 million in annual revenue")

Investors back people, not just ideas. A specific, quantified dream — paired with a personal reason — is far more credible than vague ambition.

### The Deal — What, Who, How Much

Before you can pitch you need to be able to answer three questions in one sentence each:
- **What** are you selling? (One line, plain language.)
- **Who** is the customer? (Specific segment, not "everyone".)
- **How much** are they paying, and how often? (Per-unit price, frequency, expected volume.)

Bwalya's deal: "I sell 50g portion-controlled, low-sugar groundnut snacks to primary school administrators in Lusaka. They pay ZMW 5 per unit and order 100–250 units per week, paid weekly."

In one sentence, an investor knows the product, the customer, the price, and the cadence.

### Market Segmentation and the Beachhead Market

Most early-stage entrepreneurs over-claim their market. "There are 8000 primary schools in Zambia, even 1% of them is 80 schools" is not a market plan — it is wishful thinking.

The Beachhead Market is the very first specific group of customers you will serve well, before expanding. It should be:
- Small enough that you can actually win it
- Specific enough that you can describe a typical customer in detail
- Reachable enough that you can talk to them affordably

Bwalya's Beachhead Market: 12 primary schools in Lusaka with 200–500 pupils, located within 15 km of his production site, where he already had a relationship with the head teacher or a teacher. From those 12 he aimed to win 4 in the first three months. He won 5.

### Customer Discovery — Going Out and Talking to People

Customer discovery is the practice of validating your assumptions by speaking to real customers — not by reading reports or asking friends.

Weak customer discovery: "I asked my friends if they would buy this and they said yes."

Strong customer discovery: "I visited 12 schools, met with the head teacher or bursar at each, presented samples, and asked four standard questions. 8 of 12 expressed strong interest, 5 placed pilot orders within two weeks. The 4 who declined gave consistent reasons — tied to existing supplier contracts."

Friends will lie to you to be polite. Real potential customers, when asked specific questions, will not.

### Identifying Financials and Key Value Drivers

A "financial tree" for a small business is a simple way to see what really drives the numbers:

- Revenue = Number of customers × Units per customer × Price per unit
- Profit = Revenue − Variable costs − Fixed costs

The "key value drivers" are the variables that, if you change them by a small amount, change the business by a lot. For Bwalya the two biggest drivers are (a) number of schools served and (b) units ordered per school per week. Pricing matters less because it is constrained by what schools will pay. Improving either of his two drivers by 20% is worth more than improving any other variable.

A strong student can identify the two or three key value drivers in their own business and explain why they matter more than the others.

### Pitching Tips

- Keep it short — 5 minutes for the deck, then questions
- Tell a story — start with the problem and the customer, not with yourself
- Know your numbers — be ready to defend every figure you put on a slide
- Be honest — exaggeration destroys trust the moment it is spotted
- Practise — rehearse out loud, time yourself, get feedback before the real thing
- Make the ask specific — "I am asking for ZMW 50,000 to fund three months of working capital, in exchange for a structured repayment over 12 months"

A pitch is not a presentation about your business. It is a conversation about whether you and the investor (or partner, or customer) can work together. Treat it that way.
