# CV Development

## What is a CV?

Students should understand that a CV is their primary marketing document — not just a list of facts, but a persuasive summary of their value to an employer. They should be able to explain the difference between a CV and a job application form, and understand why employers use CVs to filter candidates.

A CV (Curriculum Vitae) summarises your education, work experience, skills, and achievements. But its real job is to get you an interview. Most employers spend less than 30 seconds scanning a CV before deciding whether to read further. That means every line needs to earn its place.

A strong student can articulate: "My CV's job is to get me an interview, not get me the job."

For TEVET students, a CV is especially important because it is often the first time an employer encounters you. Unlike a large company with an online application system, many Zambian employers — workshops, hotels, farms, small businesses — will ask you to "bring your CV." What you hand them is your first impression.

## CV Structure

Students should be able to construct each section of a CV independently. They should understand not just what goes in each section, but what makes each section effective.

### Contact Information
Full name, phone number, email address, location (city, country). Students should understand that an unprofessional email address (e.g. coolguy99@email.com) can cost them an interview before anyone reads a word of their CV.

Professional format: firstname.lastname@email.com or firstnamelastname@email.com.

### Personal Statement / Professional Summary
3-4 sentences summarising who you are, what you offer, and what you are looking for. This is the hardest section for most students because it requires them to step back and describe themselves in a way that is specific and compelling.

Weak example: "I am a hardworking person looking for a job. I have many skills and I am a team player."

Strong example: "Recently qualified electrical technician with hands-on experience from a 6-month placement at Lusaka Power Systems. Skilled in residential wiring, safety compliance, and fault diagnosis. Seeking an entry-level position where I can apply my TEVET training and grow as a professional."

The weak version could describe anyone. The strong version could only describe one person — and that is the point.

### Work Experience
Job title, company, dates, key achievements. This is where many TEVET students get stuck because they think they need formal employment. They do not. Vocational placements, attachments, informal work, community roles, and family business experience all count — the key is how you frame them.

Weak example: "I worked at a garage."

Strong example: "Motor Vehicle Mechanic (Attachment) — Choma Auto Services, Jan–Jun 2025. Assisted senior mechanics with engine diagnostics and routine servicing. Independently completed brake pad replacements for 15+ vehicles. Trained to use diagnostic equipment including OBD-II scanners."

The student should leave this topic able to draft their own Work Experience section using this format, not just list what the sections are called.

For each entry, use this structure:
- Role title (use a professional title even for informal work)
- Organisation and dates
- 2-4 bullet points starting with action verbs, describing what you did and what resulted

### Education
Qualification, institution, dates, relevant coursework or achievements. For TEVET students, include your programme name, the institution, expected completion date, and any notable modules or practicals.

### Skills
Grouped by category (technical, language, computer). Include proficiency levels where appropriate. Students should distinguish between hard skills (welding, accounting software, electrical installation) and soft skills (communication, teamwork), and understand that employers value specific evidence over generic claims.

### Additional Sections (optional)
Certifications, volunteer experience, languages, references. These are optional but can strengthen a thin CV — especially volunteer work or community involvement.

## CV Writing Tips

Students should be able to apply these principles to their own CV, not just recite them. A student who understands tailoring should be able to produce two noticeably different CVs from the same experience for two different job applications.

### Tailoring
Reorder and emphasise different experiences for different job applications. A student applying for an electrical role and a customer service role should highlight different skills and experiences, even though both CVs draw from the same raw material.

### Action Verbs
Replace passive descriptions with active ones. This single change transforms a flat CV into one that shows initiative and impact.

Weak: "Was responsible for customer service."
Strong: "Handled customer enquiries, resolved complaints, and processed returns for 50+ customers daily."

More action verbs: managed, designed, trained, organised, built, repaired, analysed, delivered, supervised, coordinated, inspected, maintained, operated, installed.

### Conciseness
Ideally 1-2 pages. Every line should earn its place. If a bullet point does not help the employer understand your value for this specific role, cut it.

### Consistent Formatting
Same fonts, spacing, and bullet style throughout. A messy CV signals carelessness. Employers notice inconsistency even if they cannot articulate why the CV "feels unprofessional."

### Proofreading
Spelling and grammar errors create a poor impression. Read your CV aloud. Ask someone else to review it. Check it twice. A single typo in your email address means no one can contact you.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

Students should be able to spot these mistakes in example CVs and in their own drafts. The goal is not just knowing the list, but being able to look at a CV and say "this is weak because..." with a specific reason.

- Including irrelevant work experience that dilutes your message — every entry should support the role you are applying for
- Using vague descriptions ("did many things") instead of specific achievements with results
- Unexplained gaps in employment — better to briefly note what you were doing (studying, caregiving, volunteering) than leave a blank
- Unprofessional email address
- Making the CV too long (everything you have ever done) or too short (just a list of job titles with no detail)
- Copying someone else's CV or using a template without personalising it
- Listing duties instead of achievements

Spot the difference:
Before: "I was a waiter at a restaurant."
After: "Waiter — Manda Hill Restaurant, Lusaka (Mar–Dec 2024). Served 30+ tables per shift, trained 2 new staff members, and received Employee of the Month recognition in August."

Both describe the same job. Only one would get a second look from an employer.

## Quiz
pass_threshold: 0.7

1. Which of the following statements about CVs is most accurate?
A. A CV should include every job and experience you have ever had
B. The primary purpose of a CV is to get you an interview, not to get you the job
C. A CV is only necessary for formal corporate positions
D. Employers typically read each CV thoroughly from start to finish
Answer: B

2. A student writes "I am a hardworking team player seeking employment" as their personal statement. What is the biggest weakness?
A. It is too long for a personal statement
B. It does not mention salary expectations
C. It is generic and could describe anyone — it lacks specific skills, experience, or direction
D. It should be written in the third person
Answer: C

3. Mwila did a 3-month attachment at a hotel but was never formally employed. How should this appear on her CV?
A. It should not appear because it was not paid employment
B. She should list it under Education since it was part of her training
C. She should present it as work experience with a professional title, the hotel name, dates, and specific duties and achievements
D. She should mention it only in her cover letter, not on her CV
Answer: C

4. Which of these work experience descriptions would be most effective on a CV?
A. "Responsible for various duties in the workshop"
B. "Worked at Choma Auto Services and helped with cars"
C. "I was a hardworking and dedicated employee at an auto garage"
D. "Motor Vehicle Mechanic (Attachment) — Choma Auto Services. Independently completed brake pad replacements for 15+ vehicles and trained to use OBD-II diagnostic scanners."
Answer: D

5. A student is applying for both an electrical technician role and a customer service role. What should they do with their CV?
A. Send the same CV to both because honesty means presenting yourself consistently
B. Create two separate CVs that emphasise different skills and experiences relevant to each role
C. Only apply for the role that matches their training exactly
D. Include all their skills on one comprehensive CV and let the employer decide what is relevant
Answer: B

6. What is the most important reason to use action verbs like "managed," "repaired," and "trained" on a CV?
A. They show initiative and clearly communicate what you did, rather than passively listing responsibilities
B. Employers have software that specifically searches for action verbs
C. They make the CV longer and more detailed
D. They are required by standard CV formatting rules
Answer: A

7. A student's CV has inconsistent fonts, different bullet styles, and uneven spacing. Why does this matter?
A. It does not matter as long as the content is strong
B. Employers may reject it because formatting inconsistency suggests a lack of attention to detail and professionalism
C. It will be rejected by automated screening software
D. It only matters for design or creative industry roles
Answer: B

8. Which of these is the best way to handle a gap in your CV where you were not employed or studying?
A. Leave the gap and hope the employer does not notice
B. Invent a job to fill the gap
C. Briefly note what you were doing during that period, such as caregiving, volunteering, or self-directed learning
D. Remove dates from your CV entirely so gaps are not visible
Answer: C

9. What makes the email address "coolguy99@email.com" a problem on a CV?
A. Free email providers are not accepted by employers
B. Employers only contact candidates by phone, so the email does not matter
C. Email addresses must contain your full legal name
D. It creates an unprofessional first impression before the employer reads any of your qualifications
Answer: D

10. A student lists "communication skills" on their CV under Skills. How could this be improved?
A. Remove it because soft skills do not belong on a CV
B. Move it to the personal statement section instead
C. Change it to "excellent communication skills" for more impact
D. Replace it with a specific example in their work experience, such as "Explained technical faults to customers in non-technical language"
Answer: D
