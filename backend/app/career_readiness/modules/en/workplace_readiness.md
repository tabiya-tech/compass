# Workplace Readiness Skills

## Communication Skills

Students should be able to distinguish between effective and ineffective communication in workplace scenarios and demonstrate that they can adapt their communication style to different audiences and situations. A strong student can identify which communication mode (verbal, written, non-verbal) is most appropriate for a given workplace situation and explain why.

### Verbal Communication
The ability to speak clearly and be understood is foundational — but effective verbal communication goes beyond just talking. It means adjusting what you say and how you say it depending on who you are speaking to.

Example of audience awareness:
Explaining a fault to your supervisor: "The RCD keeps tripping on the DB board in unit 3. I've isolated the circuit and I think it's a ground fault on the geyser element."
Explaining the same fault to the homeowner: "There's an electrical safety device that keeps switching off your power. I've found the problem — it's coming from your water heater — and I'm going to fix it now. Your power should be back within the hour."

Same information, different language. Both are professional. A student should be able to do both.

Active listening is equally important — and harder than it sounds. It means letting the other person finish before you respond, asking clarifying questions when something is unclear, and confirming your understanding ("So what you're saying is..."). In a workplace, misunderstanding an instruction because you did not listen carefully can cost time, money, or safety.

### Written Communication
Professional emails and messages follow simple rules, but many new employees get them wrong.

Weak email: "hi boss im not coming today im sick"

Strong email: "Good morning Mr. Phiri. I am not feeling well today and will not be able to come in. I have asked Mwila to cover my tasks for the morning. I expect to be back tomorrow. Please let me know if there is anything urgent I should handle from home. Regards, Chanda."

The difference: the strong email is respectful in tone, provides context, shows initiative (arranged cover), and gives the manager what they need to plan the day.

Key principles:
- State your purpose early — do not bury the point
- Use professional language, even in WhatsApp messages to colleagues
- Proofread before sending — especially the recipient's name
- Respond to messages in a timely manner — even if only to say "I'll get back to you by end of day"

### Non-Verbal Communication
Your body language communicates before you say a word. Maintaining appropriate eye contact shows confidence and engagement. Slouching, checking your phone, or avoiding eye contact during a conversation signals disinterest — even if you are paying attention.

Dress code matters too. "Appropriate for your workplace" is the rule — overalls in a workshop, business casual in an office. When in doubt for your first day, dress slightly more formally than you think is necessary and adjust based on what others wear.

## Problem-Solving

Students should be able to walk through a structured problem-solving process using a real example from their experience. A strong student does not just know the steps — they can describe a time they used them (or should have used them) and explain what the outcome was.

### A Structured Approach

1. Identify the problem — define what is actually wrong, not just the symptoms. "The machine stopped working" is a symptom. "The motor overheated because the ventilation grille was blocked" is the problem.
2. Gather information — what do you know? What do you need to find out? Who can help?
3. Generate options — brainstorm at least two possible solutions before committing to one.
4. Evaluate options — consider time, cost, risk, and whether you have the authority to act.
5. Choose and implement — make a decision and act on it.
6. Review the outcome — did it work? What would you do differently next time?

### Worked Example

Situation: During your attachment at a hotel kitchen, the walk-in fridge stops cooling on a busy Friday evening. The duty manager is dealing with a large event.

Poor approach: Panic. Do nothing. Wait for someone senior to notice.

Structured approach:
1. Identify: The fridge is not cooling. You check — the compressor is running but warm air is coming out.
2. Gather information: You check the thermostat (set correctly), then notice the condenser coils are clogged with dust.
3. Options: Clean the coils yourself, call a technician, or move perishable stock to another fridge.
4. Evaluate: Cleaning the coils is quick and low-risk. Moving stock buys time. Calling a technician is expensive and slow on a Friday night.
5. Act: You clean the coils and move the most perishable items to the bar fridge as a precaution.
6. Review: The fridge starts cooling again within 30 minutes. You report to the duty manager what happened and suggest adding coil cleaning to the monthly maintenance checklist.

This is the kind of initiative that gets you noticed — and promoted.

### When to Solve It Yourself vs When to Escalate
Not every problem is yours to fix. Students should understand the difference between:
- Problems within your skill and authority — handle them and report what you did
- Problems above your skill or authority — contain the immediate risk, then escalate

Example: A loose electrical connection you know how to fix — handle it. A gas leak — secure the area and call your supervisor immediately.

## Teamwork

Students should be able to describe what effective teamwork looks like in a workplace setting and identify specific behaviours that help or harm a team. A strong student can reflect on their own teamwork habits and identify one area for improvement.

### Working Effectively with Others
Teamwork in a workplace is different from group projects at college. The stakes are higher, the people are less familiar, and you do not get to choose your team members.

Key behaviours:
- Reliability — if you say you will do something, do it by when you said you would. Nothing damages team trust faster than broken commitments.
- Communication — tell people what you are working on, flag problems early, and ask for help before you are stuck, not after.
- Respect — different people work differently. A colleague who is quiet is not lazy. A colleague who asks many questions is not slow. Assume good intent.
- Shared credit — "We figured it out together" builds a team. "I figured it out" may be true but it isolates you.
- Accountability — when you make a mistake, own it. "I wired that connection wrong, I'll fix it now" earns more respect than trying to hide the error.

### Handling Conflict
Conflict at work is normal — it is how you handle it that matters. Most workplace conflicts escalate because people avoid them until frustration boils over.

Weak approach: Ignoring the issue, complaining to other colleagues, or confronting the person in front of others.

Strong approach:
1. Address it early and privately — "Can we talk about how we're splitting the tasks? I feel like the current arrangement isn't working well for either of us."
2. Focus on the problem, not the person — "The schedule is causing delays" not "You're always late."
3. Listen to understand — the other person may have context you are missing.
4. Propose a solution — come with an idea, not just a complaint.
5. Involve a supervisor only if you cannot resolve it between yourselves.

Example: You and a colleague are sharing a workbench. They leave tools out and your workspace is always cluttered. Instead of silently resenting it or complaining to others, you say: "Hey, could we agree on a system for the tools? Maybe we each pack up at the end of our shift? It'll make it easier for both of us in the morning."

## Time Management

Students should be able to describe their own approach to managing tasks and deadlines, and identify at least one specific strategy they can apply in a workplace setting. A strong student understands that time management is not about working harder — it is about making deliberate choices about what to do first.

### Prioritisation
Not all tasks are equal. The simplest framework: do urgent and important tasks first, then important but not urgent, then urgent but not important, and eliminate tasks that are neither.

Workplace example: Your supervisor asks you to file paperwork (not urgent, not important), a client calls about a fault that needs fixing today (urgent and important), and you need to prepare for a safety inspection next week (important but not urgent).

Order: Fix the client's fault first. Start preparing for the safety inspection when you have a block of time. Fit the filing in around the edges.

### Practical Strategies
- Break large tasks into smaller steps — "Prepare for safety inspection" is overwhelming. "Check fire extinguisher dates," "update the hazard register," and "clean the electrical panel area" are doable.
- Use simple tools — a notebook, a phone calendar, or a whiteboard. The tool matters less than the habit of writing things down and checking them.
- Estimate how long tasks take — most people underestimate. If you think a job will take an hour, plan for ninety minutes. This prevents the domino effect of one late task pushing everything else back.
- Avoid multitasking — research consistently shows that switching between tasks makes you slower and more error-prone. Finish one thing, then start the next.
- Communicate early if you will miss a deadline — "I'm running behind and will need until Thursday" is professional. Missing the deadline silently is not.

## Professional Etiquette

Students should understand that professional etiquette is not about formality for its own sake — it is about building trust and showing respect in a workplace. A strong student can describe specific behaviours that create a positive professional impression and explain why they matter.

### Punctuality
Arrive on time or early. In a Zambian workplace context, "Zambian time" may be socially accepted in personal settings, but in a professional environment — especially early in your career — being late signals unreliability. If your shift starts at 07:00, being there at 07:00 means you are already changed, prepared, and ready to work — not walking through the door.

If you are going to be late due to circumstances beyond your control, communicate as early as possible.

### Taking Initiative
"What should I do next?" is fine sometimes. But "I've finished the wiring in unit 4 and I noticed the labels on the DB board in unit 5 are faded — do you want me to replace them while I'm here?" shows that you are thinking about the work, not just executing instructions.

Employers notice initiative. It is one of the main things that separates someone who stays a trainee from someone who gets offered a permanent position.

### Receiving Feedback
Feedback — including critical feedback — is how you grow. The natural response to "You did this wrong" is defensiveness. The professional response is:
1. Listen fully without interrupting
2. Ask clarifying questions if needed — "Can you show me what I should have done differently?"
3. Thank the person — even if the feedback stings
4. Apply the feedback going forward

A student who says "Thank you, I'll fix that" after making a mistake earns far more respect than one who argues, makes excuses, or goes silent.

### Boundaries Between Personal and Professional
Keep personal phone use, personal conversations, and personal problems out of work time as much as possible. This does not mean you cannot be human — but an employer who sees you on WhatsApp during work hours will question your commitment, even if you are on top of your tasks.

## Adaptability and Resilience

Students should understand that adaptability is not just "being flexible" — it is the ability to stay effective when circumstances change. A strong student can describe a time they had to adapt to something unexpected and explain what they learned from it.

### Why Adaptability Matters
In a Zambian workplace, change is constant. Power cuts disrupt schedules. Supply chains delay materials. A colleague gets sick and you have to cover unfamiliar tasks. The economy shifts and your employer restructures.

The employees who thrive are not the ones who never face problems — they are the ones who adjust without losing their effectiveness or their attitude.

### Building Resilience
Resilience is not about being tough or never struggling. It is about recovering and continuing.

Practical strategies:
- Separate what you can control from what you cannot. You cannot control a power cut. You can control whether you use the downtime to clean, organise, or plan.
- Seek learning in every setback. "What can I take from this?" is more productive than "Why does this keep happening to me?"
- Build a support network — a colleague you trust, a mentor, a former supervisor you can call for advice. You do not have to figure everything out alone.
- Take care of yourself — sleep, nutrition, and physical activity directly affect your ability to handle stress. This is not soft advice; it is practical. A technician who is exhausted makes mistakes.

### Worked Example

Situation: You start a new job and discover that the workshop uses a different wiring standard than what you learned in training.

Poor response: Complain that your training was wrong. Insist on doing things the way you learned. Refuse to adapt until someone trains you formally.

Resilient response: Ask a senior colleague to walk you through the differences. Study the new standard in your own time. Practise on non-critical tasks until you are confident. Ask for feedback. Within a few weeks, you have added a new competency — and shown your employer that you can handle change.

The first response gets you labelled as difficult. The second gets you labelled as someone worth investing in.
