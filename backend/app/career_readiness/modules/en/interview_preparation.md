# Interview Preparation

## Types of Interviews

Students should be able to identify which type of interview they are walking into and adjust their preparation accordingly. A strong student understands that "Tell me about a time when..." requires a different kind of answer than "What would you do if..." — and knows why.

### Structured Interviews
The employer asks the same predetermined questions to every candidate, often scored on a rubric. These are common in government, NGOs, and larger companies in Zambia.

Why this matters for preparation: You cannot predict the exact questions, but you can predict the format. Every answer will be compared directly against other candidates' answers to the same question. Clarity and structure in your responses become your advantage.

### Behavioural Interviews
Questions focus on past behaviour as evidence of how you will perform in the future. These typically begin with "Tell me about a time when..." or "Describe a situation where..."

The logic behind behavioural questions is simple: what you have actually done is the best predictor of what you will do. This is where the STAR method becomes essential.

Example questions:
- "Tell me about a time you had to solve a problem under pressure."
- "Describe a situation where you had to work with someone you disagreed with."
- "Give me an example of when you showed initiative."

### Situational Interviews
Hypothetical scenarios that test your judgement and problem-solving. These begin with "What would you do if..." or "Imagine that..."

The key difference from behavioural questions: you are describing what you would do, not what you did. But the strongest answers still draw on real experience.

Example: "What would you do if you noticed a safety hazard on site but your supervisor was not available?"

Weak answer: "I would deal with it."
Strong answer: "I would first secure the area to prevent anyone from being harmed, then document the hazard and report it to the next available senior staff member. During my attachment, we had a similar situation with an exposed wire, and I learned that immediate containment is always the first priority before escalating."

### Panel Interviews
Multiple interviewers at once — common for government positions and larger organisations. Students often find these intimidating, but the key principle is simple: address the whole panel, not just the person who asked the question. Make eye contact with all members when responding.

Practical tip: When you enter the room, greet each panel member. If you are offered the chance to ask questions at the end, direct different questions to different panellists — it shows you see them as individuals, not a wall of faces.

## The STAR Method

Students should be able to construct a complete STAR response from their own experience for at least three different behavioural questions. The goal is not memorising a formula but building a habit of structured, evidence-based storytelling.

STAR stands for:
- Situation — Set the scene. Where were you? What was happening?
- Task — What was your responsibility or challenge?
- Action — What specifically did you do? (Not your team — you.)
- Result — What happened? What did you learn?

### Worked Example

Question: "Tell me about a time you had to learn something quickly."

Weak answer: "I'm a fast learner. During my attachment I had to learn many new things and I always picked them up quickly."

Strong STAR answer:
- Situation: "During my first week at Choma Auto Services, a customer brought in a vehicle with an electronic fuel injection issue — something we had only briefly covered in class."
- Task: "My supervisor was busy with another job and asked me to run the initial diagnostic."
- Action: "I used the workshop's OBD-II scanner to pull the error codes, then looked up the codes in the vehicle's service manual. I identified a faulty oxygen sensor and confirmed it by checking the live data readings against the expected values."
- Result: "My supervisor confirmed my diagnosis was correct and I completed the sensor replacement that afternoon. After that, he trusted me to handle electronic diagnostics independently. I learned that I can work through unfamiliar problems if I use the tools and references available to me."

Notice: the weak answer makes a claim. The STAR answer proves the claim with evidence. Employers remember stories — they forget adjectives.

### Common STAR Mistakes
- Making it too long — each part should be 1-3 sentences, not a full paragraph
- Saying "we" instead of "I" — the interviewer wants to know what you did, not what your team did
- Forgetting the Result — this is where the impact and the learning live; never leave it out
- Using a vague or trivial example — "I worked well in a group project at college" is too generic; choose moments that had real stakes

### Building Your STAR Bank
Before any interview, prepare 4-5 stories from your experience that demonstrate different qualities: problem-solving, teamwork, initiative, learning, handling pressure. Each story can often be adapted to answer several different questions.

Sources for stories:
- Your TEVET placement or attachment
- Practical workshops and group projects during training
- Part-time or informal work
- Community or volunteer involvement
- A personal challenge you overcame

## Common Interview Questions

Students should be able to answer each of these question types with a structured, specific response — not a rehearsed script, but a prepared framework they can adapt. The goal is to think of preparation as having ingredients ready, not memorising a recipe.

### "Tell Me About Yourself"
This is not an invitation to recite your life story. It is a 60-second professional summary — similar to the professional introduction from Module 1, but tailored to the specific role.

Structure: Present → Past → Future
- Present: What you are doing now (your training, your current situation)
- Past: Relevant experience that has prepared you
- Future: Why you are here and what you are looking for

Example: "I'm currently completing my Craft Certificate in Electrical Engineering at Evelyn Hone College. Over the past year, I completed a six-month attachment at Lusaka Power Systems where I focused on residential fault diagnosis and wiring installations. That experience confirmed that hands-on electrical work is where I want to build my career, and I'm excited about this opportunity because your company's focus on rural electrification aligns with where I want to make an impact."

### "What Are Your Strengths and Weaknesses?"
For strengths: pick one or two that are directly relevant to the role and back them up with evidence.

Weak: "I'm hardworking and punctual."
Strong: "One of my strengths is methodical troubleshooting. During my attachment, I was often asked to diagnose faults that other trainees couldn't identify, because I follow a systematic process rather than guessing."

For weaknesses: choose something genuine but manageable, and explain what you are doing about it.

Weak: "I'm a perfectionist" (this sounds rehearsed and insincere).
Strong: "I sometimes spend too long on a task trying to get it exactly right when 'good enough' would be fine. I've been working on this by setting time limits for myself and checking with my supervisor when I'm unsure if something needs more attention."

### "Why Do You Want This Job?"
The answer should connect three things: what you know about the company, what the role involves, and why that matches your skills and goals.

Weak: "I need a job and your company is well known."
Strong: "I want to work at ZESCO because you're leading Zambia's rural electrification programme, which is exactly the kind of work I trained for. The Trainee Technician role would let me apply my residential wiring experience while learning about distribution systems at a larger scale."

### Behavioural Questions
Use STAR (covered above). Common themes: teamwork, conflict, pressure, initiative, learning from failure.

### Questions You Should Ask the Interviewer
Always prepare 2-3 questions. This shows genuine interest and helps you evaluate whether the job is right for you.

Good questions:
- "What does a typical day look like in this role?"
- "What qualities make someone successful in this position?"
- "Are there opportunities for further training or professional development?"

Bad questions (for a first interview):
- "How much does the job pay?" (wait for the employer to raise this)
- "How soon can I get promoted?"
- "Do I have to work weekends?"

## Preparation Checklist

Students should understand that interview preparation is not optional — it is what separates candidates who get offers from candidates who were equally qualified but did not prepare. A strong student can describe their complete preparation routine.

Before the interview:
1. Research the company — what they do, their values, recent news or projects. Check their website and social media.
2. Re-read the job posting — identify the key requirements and match each one to something in your experience.
3. Prepare 4-5 STAR stories covering different qualities (problem-solving, teamwork, initiative, learning, handling pressure).
4. Practise your "Tell me about yourself" answer until it feels natural, not memorised.
5. Prepare 2-3 questions to ask the interviewer.
6. Plan your outfit — clean, professional, appropriate for the industry. When in doubt, dress slightly more formally than you think is necessary.
7. Plan your route — know exactly where the interview is and how long it takes to get there. Aim to arrive 10-15 minutes early.
8. Bring copies of your CV, any certificates or references requested, a notebook, and a pen.

The night before:
- Lay out your clothes and documents
- Get enough sleep
- Review your key stories one final time — but do not cram. If you are not prepared the night before, last-minute panic will not help.

## During the Interview

Students should understand that interview performance is not just about what you say — it is about how you come across as a person. A strong student can describe the non-verbal behaviours that create a positive impression and explain why "I'll just be myself" is not a complete strategy.

### First Impressions
- Arrive 10-15 minutes early. Late arrival is almost impossible to recover from.
- Greet everyone politely — including receptionists and other staff. Employers sometimes ask their team for impressions.
- Offer a firm handshake and make eye contact.
- Sit up straight and keep an open posture. Crossed arms and slouching signal discomfort or disinterest.

### During Your Answers
- Listen carefully to the full question before responding. If you are unsure what they are asking, it is better to say "Could you clarify what you mean?" than to answer the wrong question.
- Take a moment to think. A 3-second pause before a structured answer is far better than rushing into a rambling response.
- Be honest. If you do not know something, say so — and explain how you would find out. "I haven't worked with that specific system, but I'm comfortable learning new tools. During my attachment, I taught myself to use the OBD-II scanner within my first week."
- Be specific. Every answer should include at least one concrete detail from your experience.

### Handling Nerves
Nerves are normal — even experienced professionals feel them. The difference is that prepared candidates have something solid to fall back on.

Strategies:
- Preparation is the single best antidote to nerves. If you have your stories ready, you will not freeze.
- Breathe. If you feel your heart racing, take one slow breath before your next answer.
- Remember that the interviewer wants you to succeed — they have a vacancy to fill and they are hoping you are the right person.
- If you stumble on an answer, it is fine to say "Let me start that again" — composure under pressure is itself impressive.

### Closing the Interview
- Thank the interviewer for their time.
- Reiterate your interest in the role briefly: "Thank you for this conversation. I'm very interested in this role and I'm confident my experience at Lusaka Power Systems has prepared me well."
- Ask about next steps if they have not mentioned them: "When can I expect to hear about the outcome?"
