# Cover Letter & Motivation Statement

## What is a Cover Letter?

Students should understand that a cover letter is not a summary of their CV — it is a persuasive argument for why they specifically should be considered for a specific role. A strong student can explain the difference between a cover letter and a CV and articulate why sending a CV without a cover letter is a missed opportunity.

A cover letter is a one-page document that accompanies your CV. While your CV says "here is what I have done," your cover letter says "here is why what I have done matters for this role." It is your chance to speak directly to the employer as a person, not just a list of qualifications.

Think of it this way: your CV gets you into the pile. Your cover letter gets you to the top of the pile.

For TEVET students, cover letters are especially valuable because they let you explain context that a CV cannot. A CV shows a 6-month attachment — a cover letter explains what you learned, why you are passionate about the field, and how you will bring that energy to the employer's team.

Many Zambian employers — particularly NGOs, government agencies, and larger companies — specifically request a cover letter or motivation statement. Even when it is not required, including one signals professionalism and genuine interest.

## Cover Letter Structure

Students should be able to write each section of a cover letter independently and understand the purpose each section serves. A student who completes this topic should be able to draft a complete cover letter for a real or hypothetical job posting.

### Header
Your contact details, the date, and the employer's contact details. This mirrors formal business letter format and shows professionalism.

### Opening Paragraph
State the position you are applying for, where you saw the opportunity, and one compelling reason you are a strong candidate. The opening must grab attention — if the first sentence is boring, the rest may not be read.

Weak opening: "I am writing to apply for the position advertised on your website. I believe I am a good candidate."

Strong opening: "I am applying for the Electrical Technician Trainee position advertised on the ZESCO careers page. With a Craft Certificate in Electrical Engineering from Evelyn Hone College and six months of hands-on residential wiring experience at Lusaka Power Systems, I am confident I can contribute to your team from day one."

The weak version tells the employer nothing they could not guess. The strong version gives them three concrete reasons to keep reading in two sentences.

### Body Paragraphs (1-2)
This is the core of your argument. Each paragraph should make a specific point supported by evidence from your experience.

Paragraph 1 — Your strongest relevant qualification or experience:
Pick the one thing from your background that is most relevant to this role and expand on it with a specific example.

Example: "During my attachment at Lusaka Power Systems, I was responsible for conducting fault diagnosis on residential electrical systems. Over six months, I independently resolved wiring faults for over 40 households and was trusted to supervise two junior trainees during their orientation. This experience taught me to work methodically under pressure and to communicate technical issues clearly to non-technical customers."

Paragraph 2 — Why this employer and what you bring:
Show that you have researched the company and explain how your goals align with theirs.

Example: "I am drawn to ZESCO's commitment to expanding rural electrification across Zambia. Having grown up in a community that gained reliable electricity only recently, I understand the impact this work has on people's lives. I want to be part of a team that is making that difference on a national scale."

### Closing Paragraph
Restate your interest, thank the reader, and include a call to action.

Example: "Thank you for considering my application. I would welcome the opportunity to discuss how my training and placement experience have prepared me for this role. I am available for an interview at your convenience and can be reached at 097X XXX XXX or chanda.mulenga@email.com."

## Motivation Statement vs Cover Letter

Students should understand when each document is appropriate and how they differ in focus. A student who completes this topic should be able to identify whether a given application requires a cover letter, a motivation statement, or either.

A cover letter focuses on: why you are right for this specific role, based on your skills and experience.

A motivation statement focuses on: why you want this opportunity, what drives you, and what you hope to contribute and gain. It is more personal and forward-looking.

Motivation statements are commonly requested for:
- Academic programmes and scholarships
- NGO and development sector positions
- Volunteer and fellowship programmes
- Government training schemes

Key difference in tone:

Cover letter approach: "My six months of residential wiring experience at Lusaka Power Systems make me a strong candidate for your Electrical Technician Trainee position."

Motivation statement approach: "Growing up in a community that lacked reliable electricity shaped my decision to study electrical engineering. I want to contribute to Zambia's rural electrification goals, and your graduate programme would give me the technical depth and field experience to do that effectively."

Both are professional. The cover letter leads with qualifications. The motivation statement leads with purpose.

A common mistake is writing a motivation statement that is entirely about what you want ("I want to develop my career, gain experience, and earn a good salary") without explaining what you offer. The best motivation statements balance personal motivation with the value you bring.

## Writing Tips

Students should be able to apply these principles to produce a cover letter that feels personal and specific, not like a template. A strong student can look at a job posting and identify which of their experiences to highlight and how to connect them to the employer's needs.

### Research the Employer
Reference something specific about the organisation — their mission, a recent project, a value you share. This shows genuine interest and takes your letter from generic to targeted.

Generic: "I would like to work for your company."
Targeted: "I was impressed by First Quantum's investment in training local technicians at Kansanshi Mine, and I want to be part of an organisation that values developing Zambian talent."

### Be Specific
Every claim should have evidence behind it. If you say you are a problem-solver, describe a problem you solved.

Generic: "I am a dedicated and skilled worker."
Specific: "During my placement, I identified a recurring fault in the workshop's wiring that had been causing intermittent power cuts. I traced the issue to a loose neutral connection and repaired it, eliminating a problem that had disrupted work for weeks."

### Match Keywords from the Job Description
If the posting asks for "experience with safety compliance," use that exact phrase when describing your relevant experience. This shows you have read the requirements carefully and makes it easy for the employer to see the match.

### Address It to a Specific Person
"Dear Ms. Banda" is always stronger than "To Whom It May Concern." If the posting does not name a contact, try calling the organisation or checking their website. If you truly cannot find a name, "Dear Hiring Manager" is acceptable.

### Keep It to One Page
A cover letter should be 3-4 paragraphs on a single page. If it spills onto a second page, cut ruthlessly. Every sentence must earn its place.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

Students should be able to identify these mistakes in sample letters and in their own drafts. The goal is a critical eye, not just a memorised list.

- Repeating your CV word for word — the cover letter should add context and narrative, not duplicate information the employer can already see on your CV
- Using a generic template without customisation — employers can tell when a letter was not written for them, and it signals low effort
- Focusing only on what you want ("I want to gain experience and develop my career") without explaining what you offer the employer
- Forgetting to proofread — a misspelled company name or the wrong employer's name left in from a previous application is one of the most damaging mistakes
- Not following application instructions — if the posting says "email your CV and motivation statement as a single PDF," doing it differently suggests you cannot follow directions
- Writing too much — a three-page cover letter will not be read; discipline shows professionalism

Spot the difference:
Before: "Dear Sir/Madam, I am writing to apply for a job at your company. I am a hardworking individual with good skills. Please find attached my CV. I look forward to hearing from you."

After: "Dear Ms. Banda, I am applying for the Trainee Electrician position at Kafue Electrical Services, advertised on the TEVETA job board. During my Craft Certificate training at Lusaka TEVET College, I completed a six-month attachment where I independently handled residential fault diagnosis for 40+ households. I am excited about the opportunity to bring this hands-on experience to your team and to continue developing my skills under the mentorship your programme offers."

The first version could be sent to any company for any role. The second could only be sent to one company for one role — and that is what makes it effective.
