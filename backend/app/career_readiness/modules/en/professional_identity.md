# Professional Identity & Skills Mapping

## What is Professional Identity?

Students should be able to explain what professional identity means in their own words and describe how it shapes career decisions. A strong student can articulate the difference between "what I do" and "who I am professionally" — and understand that professional identity is something they already have, even before formal employment.

Professional identity is not just a job title. It is the combination of your values, skills, experiences, and goals that defines how you see yourself in the working world and how others perceive you. For TEVET students, professional identity often begins forming during training — the trade you chose, the problems you enjoy solving, the standards you hold yourself to.

A student who says "I'm studying electrical engineering" has a label. A student who says "I'm someone who enjoys understanding how systems work and making sure they're safe — that's why I chose electrical" has a professional identity.

Why it matters:
- Employers hire people, not qualification certificates. Your identity is what makes you memorable beyond your grades.
- A clear professional identity helps you make better career decisions — you can evaluate whether a job fits who you are, not just whether you qualify on paper.
- It gives you confidence in interviews, networking, and workplace interactions.

Common misconception: "I don't have a professional identity yet because I haven't worked." Students should recognise that their training choices, placement experiences, volunteer work, and even how they approach problems in class all contribute to professional identity.

## Types of Skills

Students should be able to categorise their own skills into the three types below and give at least two concrete examples of each from their own experience. They should understand that employers value all three types and that transferable skills are often the deciding factor between candidates with similar technical qualifications.

### Technical Skills
Specific, measurable abilities tied to a trade or profession. These are what your TEVET training primarily develops.

Examples for TEVET students:
- Electrical: residential wiring, circuit testing, fault diagnosis, reading wiring diagrams
- Automotive: engine diagnostics, brake system repair, wheel alignment
- Hospitality: food preparation, menu costing, front desk operations
- Agriculture: crop management, irrigation systems, soil testing
- ICT: network setup, hardware troubleshooting, database management

### Knowledge-Based Skills
Understanding gained through education and experience in specific domains. These are "what you know" rather than "what you can do with your hands."

Examples:
- Safety regulations and compliance standards (e.g. ZEMA environmental standards, ZABS quality standards)
- Industry-specific theory (e.g. Ohm's law for electrical students, food hygiene regulations for hospitality)
- Understanding of tools, materials, and their appropriate applications
- Awareness of industry trends and new technologies in your field

### Transferable Skills
Portable abilities that apply across jobs, industries, and life contexts. Students often undervalue these because they feel "obvious" — but they are what employers mention most in job descriptions.

Examples and how to spot them:
- Communication — Have you explained a technical process to someone who isn't in your field? That's communication.
- Problem-solving — Have you figured out why something wasn't working during a practical session? That's problem-solving.
- Teamwork — Have you coordinated with classmates on a group project or shared tools during a workshop? That's teamwork.
- Time management — Have you balanced coursework, placement duties, and personal responsibilities? That's time management.
- Leadership — Have you helped a classmate who was struggling, or taken charge when a group had no direction? That's leadership.

Weak skills identification: "I have communication skills and I'm a team player."
Strong skills identification: "During my attachment at Choma Auto Services, I explained brake system faults to customers in simple language so they understood what repairs were needed and why. I also coordinated with two other apprentices to manage the workshop schedule when our supervisor was away."

## How to Identify Your Skills

Students should be able to use at least two of the methods below to surface skills they didn't initially recognise. The goal is not to list skills from a textbook — it is to mine their own experience for evidence. A student who completes this topic should have identified at least 5-8 genuine skills with real examples behind each one.

### Method 1: Experience Audit
Walk through every significant experience — not just paid work, but training practicals, placements, volunteer work, community roles, family responsibilities, and personal projects.

For each experience, ask:
- What did I actually do? (not my title, but my activities)
- What was hard about it? What did I have to learn or figure out?
- What did other people rely on me for?

Example: A student who helped her mother run a market stall might not think of that as "work experience," but it involved stock management, cash handling, customer service, negotiation, and working long hours under pressure.

### Method 2: Feedback from Others
Ask classmates, instructors, supervisors from placements, family members, and friends: "What do you think I'm good at?" People around you often see strengths you take for granted.

### Method 3: The Action Verb Test
Go through this list and circle every verb that describes something you have done:
managed, repaired, built, organised, trained, designed, calculated, inspected, communicated, supervised, installed, tested, analysed, cleaned, maintained, operated, served, delivered, coordinated, solved

Each circled verb is a skill. Now attach a specific example to it.

### Method 4: Problem Reflection
Think about times something went wrong — a broken machine, a disagreement in a group, a tight deadline. How did you handle it? Problem moments reveal skills more clearly than routine work.

## Articulating Your Professional Identity

Students should be able to introduce themselves professionally in 30 seconds in a way that is specific, authentic, and relevant to their audience. They should understand that articulation is a skill that improves with practice, not something you either "have" or "don't have."

The goal is a professional introduction that answers three questions:
1. Who are you? (your background and training)
2. What can you do? (your strongest, most relevant skills with evidence)
3. What are you looking for? (your direction and motivation)

Weak example: "My name is Chanda. I am a student at Evelyn Hone College. I am hardworking and I am looking for a job."

Strong example: "My name is Chanda Mulenga. I'm completing my Craft Certificate in Electrical Engineering at Evelyn Hone College, where I've focused on residential wiring and safety compliance. During my six-month attachment at Lusaka Power Systems, I independently handled fault diagnosis for residential customers and was trusted to supervise two junior trainees. I'm looking for an entry-level position where I can apply my hands-on experience and continue developing my skills in power distribution."

Key principles:
- Lead with specifics, not generalities. "Hardworking" tells an employer nothing. "Independently handled fault diagnosis" tells them exactly what you can do.
- Tailor to your audience. Introducing yourself at a mining industry career fair is different from introducing yourself to a neighbourhood electrician who might hire you.
- Be honest about your level. "I'm an experienced technician" when you have a 6-month attachment will damage trust. "I have hands-on training and I'm eager to learn on the job" is both honest and appealing.
- Include evidence. Every claim should have a story behind it, even if you don't tell the full story every time.

Practice format — the 30-second professional introduction:
"I'm [name], a [qualification/field] student at [institution]. During my training, I've developed skills in [2-3 specific skills], which I applied during [placement/project/experience]. I'm looking for [type of opportunity] where I can [what you want to contribute and develop]."

1. A student says "I'm a hardworking team player looking for any opportunity." What is the main problem with this as a professional introduction?
A. It is too short to be effective
B. It should include salary expectations
C. It uses generic claims without specific evidence or direction
D. It does not mention their qualification
Answer: C

2. Thandi helped her mother run a market stall for two years before starting her TEVET programme. Which statement best describes the professional value of this experience?
A. It is only relevant if she wants to work in retail
B. It demonstrates skills like cash handling, customer service, and stock management that transfer to many workplaces
C. She should leave it off her professional profile because it was a family business
D. It has no professional value because it was not formal employment
Answer: B

3. What is the key difference between technical skills and transferable skills?
A. Transferable skills can only be learned in school
B. Technical skills are more important than transferable skills
C. Technical skills are easy to learn while transferable skills take years to develop
D. Technical skills are specific to a trade or profession, while transferable skills apply across many jobs and industries
Answer: D

4. A student on an electrical engineering placement was asked to explain a wiring fault to a homeowner in simple terms. Which types of skills did this require?
A. Only technical skills
B. Only knowledge-based skills
C. Both technical skills (fault diagnosis) and transferable skills (communication)
D. Only transferable skills
Answer: C

5. Which of the following is a knowledge-based skill rather than a technical skill?
A. Repairing a motor engine
B. Installing a circuit breaker
C. Operating a welding machine
D. Understanding safety compliance regulations
Answer: D

6. Why is it important to tailor your professional introduction to your audience?
A. Because tailoring makes you appear more experienced than you are
B. Because you need a longer introduction for senior people
C. Because you should tell each employer what they want to hear, even if it is not true
D. Because different audiences value different aspects of your experience, and relevance builds connection
Answer: D

7. A student says they have "no skills" because they have never had a formal job. What is the best advice?
A. They should focus only on the technical skills from their TEVET programme
B. They should copy skills from a job posting that interests them
C. They should review all their experiences — training, volunteering, community roles, personal projects — because skills come from many sources, not just employment
D. They should wait until they have work experience before trying to identify skills
Answer: C

8. Which of these is the strongest way to describe a transferable skill?
A. "Communication is one of my key strengths"
B. "I explained brake system faults to customers in simple language so they understood what repairs were needed"
C. "I have good communication skills"
D. "I communicate well with people"
Answer: B

9. A student is preparing for a career fair in the mining sector but trained in electrical engineering. What should they emphasise in their introduction?
A. Everything they learned in their programme equally
B. Their interest in mining, even though they have no relevant skills to mention
C. Only their academic grades
D. The specific skills from their training that are relevant to mining operations, such as power systems and safety compliance

10. What is the most important thing a professional identity should communicate to an employer?
